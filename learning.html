Cash Back Cards

What This Is: Cash back cards reward you with real money back on your spending. One dollar spent = a few cents returned. Simple, automatic, and no made-up point systems to figure out.

Why This Matters: Cash back has a set value—you always know what you're getting. A flat 2% back on everything is like an instant discount on every purchase, and some cards even give extra back on things like groceries, gas, or entertainment.

Practical Advice
At the bare minimum, use a cash back card instead of a debit card—2% back is free money. If you want guaranteed rewards with zero effort, this is the way to go.

Travel Cards

What This Is: Travel cards earn points instead of cash back. Every bank, airline, and hotel has their own name for them—"miles," "rewards," "stars"—but they all work the same way: they're points.

Why This Matters: Unlike cash back, points don't have a set value. Sometimes they can be worth much more if you redeem them the right way, but that takes extra work, planning, and keeping track of rules. The upside: more potential value. The downside: more effort.

Practical Advice
Travel cards can give you outsized rewards, but only if you're willing to play the game. If you'd rather keep it simple, stick with cash back.

Points (The Made-Up Currency)

What it is: Travel cards earn points instead of cash back. Each bank, airline, or hotel calls them something different, but they're all just points. They can be worth as little as half a cent or 10x more, depending on how you use them.

Why it matters: Unlike cash back, points don't have a fixed value. That means your rewards can be really good—or really disappointing—depending on how you redeem them. Knowing this is step one.

Practical Advice
Points are confusing on purpose. Start with fixed-value points for simplicity, move to transferable points if you want to learn the game.

Effective Annual Fees

What it is: Most travel cards charge an annual fee but promise to "offset" it with credits (like travel, food, or streaming).

Why it matters: The key is organic spend—using credits on things you'd buy anyway. If you're stretching to buy stuff just to "use your credits," you're not saving money—you're overspending to justify your card.

Practical Advice
Only count credits you'd naturally use. If you wouldn't pay for it without the card, it's not really offsetting the fee.

What it is: How many points you earn in each spending category—dining, groceries, gas, etc.

Why it matters: Using multiple cards can boost rewards, but it also adds complexity. Know your personality—some people love juggling categories, others just want one card. Flat-rate cards that earn the same everywhere keep things simple.

Practical Advice
Don't overcomplicate things. Pick a flat-rate card for simplicity or multiple cards if you actually enjoy tracking categories.

What it is: How much your points are worth when you cash them in. This is what people mean when they talk about "cents per point."

Why it matters: It's confusing because point values change. A cash back card is always the same (2% back is 2%), but travel points might be worth 0.5 cents one day and 5 cents the next.

Practical Advice
Think of redemption rates as the swing factor. Cash back = steady and predictable. Points = can be great, but only if you use them well.

Churning (Expert Level)

What it is: Churning is the strategy of opening credit cards just for the sign-up bonuses, then canceling the card after the first year. You basically pay one year of an annual fee in exchange for a massive pile of points.

Why it matters: Done well, it can unlock incredible value—multiple free flights, hotel stays, even luxury trips. Done poorly, it can hurt your credit, waste money on fees, or get you flagged by banks.

Practical Advice
This is serious expert-level stuff. Don't even consider it until you've mastered the basics. For most people, a couple of well-timed sign-up bonuses is plenty. Churning is for the pros who treat credit cards like a part-time job.

Types of Cash Back Cards

Every cash back card falls into one of these categories. Understanding the trade-offs is crucial for picking the right strategy.

Level 3 - Deep Dive on Cash Back Cards

Practical Explanation

Every cash back card falls into one of three main types: flat rate, category, or rotating. The type you choose fundamentally changes how you need to think about spending and the mental overhead required.

Flat rate cards give you the same percentage on everything (1.5-2%). Category cards give higher rates (3-6%) on specific types of purchases. Rotating cards offer the highest rates (usually 5%) but only on categories that change every quarter.

Flat Rate Cards

Get 1.5-2% back on everything, always. The simplest option with no categories to track, no quarterly activations, just consistent rewards.

Click to learn more

Best For

People who want to set it and forget it. If you don't want to think about which card to use or track quarterly categories, flat rate cards are perfect. They're also ideal for people who have diverse spending that doesn't fit neatly into specific categories.

The math is simple: every purchase earns the same rate. No need to memorize bonus categories or activate quarterly rewards. You can literally use this card for everything and maximize your rewards without any mental overhead.

Category Cards

Get 3-6% on specific categories, 1% on everything else. Higher rewards but requires remembering which card to use when. Only worth it for heavy category spenders.

Click to learn more

Best For

Heavy spenders in specific categories. If you spend $300+ per month on groceries, $200+ per month on gas, or $400+ per month on dining, category cards can provide significantly higher rewards than flat rate cards.

The catch: you need to remember which card to use when. Groceries on the grocery card, gas on the gas card, everything else on your flat rate card. This requires more mental overhead but can increase your total rewards by 50-100% if you spend heavily in bonus categories.

Rotating Categories

Get 5% on categories that change quarterly. Highest potential rewards but requires tracking quarterly changes and activating bonuses. High maintenance.

Click to learn more

Best For

Organized people who don't mind complexity. If you enjoy optimizing your spending, don't mind tracking quarterly calendar changes, and remember to activate bonuses every three months, rotating cards offer the highest cash back rates.

The reality: most people forget to activate the quarterly bonuses or lose track of what the current category is. These cards only make sense if you're genuinely organized and committed to the maintenance they require. Otherwise, you'll earn less than a simple flat rate card.

Break-Even Analysis: When is a Fee Worth It?

Here's how to figure out if a category card with an annual fee beats a free 2% flat-rate card. Let's use the Blue Cash Preferred ($95 fee, 6% on groceries) as an example.

The BCP earns 4% more on groceries than a 2% card (6% - 2% = 4%). To find the spending needed to cover the $95 fee with that extra 4%, the calculation is:

$$ \text{Break-Even Spending} = \frac{\text{Annual Fee}}{\text{Bonus Reward Rate}} = \frac{\$95}{0.04} = \$2,375 $$

You need to spend $2,375 per year (or about $198 per month) on groceries just to break even with a simple, no-fee 2% card. If you spend more than that, the fee card is better. If you spend less, the no-fee card wins.

Level 3 - Deep Dive on Points

Practical Explanation

Points are a made-up currency that can be worth anywhere from 0.5 cents to 10+ cents depending on how you use them. Unlike cash back, points don't have a fixed value - their worth changes based on how you redeem them.

There are three main types of points: fixed-value (always worth 1 cent), transferable (can be moved to airline/hotel partners), and brand-locked (only work with one company). Each requires different levels of expertise to maximize value.

Fixed-Value Points

These are the simplest travel points. The math is straightforward: if you have 50,000 points, you can book $500 worth of travel. No complicated ratios or award charts.

Transferable Points

Where things get interesting - and complicated. These points can be transferred to airline and hotel partners, and their value changes dramatically depending on where you transfer them.

Click to learn more

Major Transferable Point Systems

Chase Ultimate Rewards, Amex Membership Rewards, and Citi ThankYou Points all fall into this category. Here's how the value works: you might transfer 100,000 Chase points to Hyatt and get $2,000+ worth of hotel stays, or transfer them to United and get $800 worth of flights. The same points, completely different values.

This is why travel nerds love transferable points - they offer the potential for much higher value than fixed-value points. But here's the catch: you have to actually know what you're doing. You need to understand award charts, transfer ratios, blackout dates, and availability patterns.

Brand-Locked Points

The most restrictive type of travel currency. Delta SkyMiles only work with Delta, Marriott Bonvoy only works with Marriott, and so on. These points are designed to lock you into one brand.

Click to learn more

The Brand-Locked Trap

The problem with brand-locked points is that you're at the mercy of one company's pricing and availability. If Delta decides to raise their award prices (which they do regularly), your SkyMiles become worth less overnight. If Marriott doesn't have availability at the hotel you want, your Bonvoy points are useless.

These points only make sense if you're extremely loyal to one brand and travel frequently enough to justify the loyalty. For everyone else, they're usually a trap that limits your options and often provides worse value than simpler alternatives.

How to Choose the Right Point System

The key to choosing the right point system is being honest about your willingness to learn and optimize. Don't get caught up in theoretical maximum values.

Choose Your Complexity Level

If you want to set up your cards and never think about them again, go with fixed-value points. If you enjoy learning complex systems and want to maximize value, transferable points might be worth the effort.

But here's the most important thing: don't get caught up in theoretical maximum values. A point is only worth what you can actually get for it. If you're not willing to learn transfer partners and deal with blackout dates, your points are probably worth 1 cent each. And that's totally fine.

Remember: the goal is to get value from your credit cards, not to become a travel hacker. Choose the complexity level that matches your personality and time availability. There's no prize for making things more complicated than they need to be.

Effective Annual Fees: The "Offset" Scam Explained

Level 3 - Deep Dive on Annual Fees

Practical Explanation

When credit card companies talk about "offsetting" your annual fee, they're using marketing math that would make a used car salesman blush. The effective annual fee is what the card actually costs you after using all the credits and benefits they offer.

Here's the catch: you have to actually use those credits for things you would buy anyway. A credit is only valuable if you would have spent that money anyway. If you never use Uber and already have Netflix, those credits are worth exactly $0 to you.

Real Example: Amex Platinum Math

Let's say you're already spending $200/month on Uber rides for work, $100/month on streaming services, and you fly frequently enough that you'll use the $200 airline fee credit. In this case, the Amex Platinum's effective annual fee really does become $255 ($695 - $200 - $240 - $200).

But notice the key word: "already." You were spending this money before you got the card. The card isn't saving you money - it's just giving you back some of what you were already spending. The question becomes: are the other benefits worth the remaining $255?

The Step-by-Step Process

Here's how to actually calculate whether a card makes sense for you. First, list out all the credits and benefits the card offers. Then, for each one, ask yourself: "Would I spend this money anyway?"

If the answer is yes, that credit counts toward offsetting your fee. If the answer is no, that credit is worth $0. Add up only the credits you would actually use, subtract that from the annual fee, and that's your real effective annual fee.

The Main Advantages

Fixed point values: A Chase point is always worth 1 cent in the portal (or 1.25-1.5 cents if you have the right card). You won't get the amazing 2+ cent values that transfer partners can offer, but you also won't get the 0.5 cent values that can happen with airline transfers.
